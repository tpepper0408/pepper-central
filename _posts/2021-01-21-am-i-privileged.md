## A thought experiment
I think most people will know the story of Sisyphus, or at least his punishment. He was a King of Corinth who cheated death twice and was punished by being cursed to roll a boulder up a hill, but when he reached the top it would roll to the bottom.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/43/Punishment_sisyph.jpg)

For the sake of argument, don’t worry about the never-ending nature of his punishment, just imagine yourself climbing a hill and pushing a boulder up it. The top of that hill is a goal, something that you are aiming for in life. You’re not the only person on the hill, pushing towards that same goal, you can see others doing the same. It’s not a race to the top, there’s space up there for a lot of people. All you have to do is keep pushing ahead until you reach the top.

But that’s not all, you notice that your path to the top is pretty well-maintained, as far as you can see at least. It might not be the easiest path, but it’s been cleared somewhat by people before you. They cleared it because you are like them; they’ve made it easier for people like you to push that boulder. You never asked to be on this particular path to the top but you’re glad that someone has tread it before. You can't see the whole path but you have a good starting point.

And what's more, you have hands on your shoulders stopping you feeling too much strain from boulder-pushing. You’ve got people behind you, pushing you along. You’ve also got pretty good shoes, they give a good grip, and gloves to stop your hands from getting scrapes and blisters. You're pretty well-equipped, it turns out.

All in all, you’re having a better time of it than Sisyphus. What we need to do is get to the top of that hill.

### Adding flesh to the bones
#### The path - forging forward
The path ahead is the societal structures set in place to aid you, the laws, institutions and cultural norms that make the your path easier to follow. Those people that cleared the way were pioneer, activists or ancestors that rearranged the path to make it easier for you to follow. The path is not directly in your control and you are not to blame for those earlier people clearing the way.

You can't see the whole path, in fact to some extent you are ignorant of what is further ahead and how your path is different from others. Society is a complex beast and the societal norms that benefit you right now may not be there tomorrow. You are not even aware of some of the structures that exist to benefit you or be a detriment to you, maybe because you haven't tried to look, maybe because the very nature of those things is to appear invisible.

#### The hands - keeping you afloat
The hands holding you up and the equipment you have are the factors that keep you afloat and allow you to push on to achieve what you set out to do. They are financial stability, food, friends and family, confidence, mental and physical health, physical ability. You can think of them like Maslow's hierarchy of needs, and everyone has a varying degree of these depending on their background and situation. To some extent you have control over these factors and others you don’t, but you are situated on that hill with a set of conditions that are yours. You're not even aware of all the things that are helping you push that boulder.

## Kate Bush
You can hear the throes of guttural 80s new wave (youtube video provided). The words float over to you:
> Say If I only could, I’d make a deal with God and I’d get him to swap our places; and be running up that road, be running up that hill with no problems.

{% include video.html id="wp43OdtAAkM" %}

Haunting. But it does raise the question: Who would want to swap?

Returning to the hill, turn your attention to the other people on that hill. Kate Bush is there, grunting away.

Kate thinks that you are in a better position than her, that is clear. She thinks that if she were afforded the same situation and the same path as you then she would be running up the hill. But you have also have a boulder, it's not easy for you to push it; you might have some things better than her but it's not your fault if that's the case.

It could just be that Kate is complaining in order to take you down a peg, she's not actually got it worse! How dare she suggest otherwise!

Some of the other people on that hill, pushing their own boulders, seem to be better off than you. Better equipment, strong people behind them, easier paths. Whilst others are not as lucky as you. You've got a situation and a path. You can compare the differences and hardships of others on that hill to you, even if you can't see the whole picture.

However, you can only see the broad strokes of other's paths and their situation, not the detail. Much like you have blind spots in your path, you have even less of a feel for others. If Kate wanted to swap with you, you might have to trust her assessment. If you wanted to know more, you'd have to ask.

### What is privilege?
Privilege in the broadest sense is having power based upon one's identity, that is, rights, benefits and advantages exclusively granted to a someone as a member of a particular group of people.

![](https://static.wixstatic.com/media/ccc935_d86787a1ac994575a1395aa2540ffc64~mv2.jpg/v1/fill/w_400,h_400,al_c,q_90/ccc935_d86787a1ac994575a1395aa2540ffc64~mv2.jpg)


You will have experienced the effects of privilege - knowingly or unknowingly - favourable or unfavourable. If you have it, you're often oblivious to it, and you often assume that the benefits that are afforded to you are due to your own efforts. Those with less privilege can also be thought to be less able, less intelligent or to have put in less effort. Admitting you have privilege is often very difficult because you are, in effect, saying that you did not earn the gifts you have fairly.

This quote from Peggy McIntosh goes some way to explain what it feels like to have privilege.
> “I have come to see white privilege as an invisible package of unearned assets that I can count on cashing in each day, but about which I was ‘meant’ to remain oblivious…. White privilege is like an invisible weightless knapsack of special provisions, maps, passports, codebooks, visas, clothes, tools, and blank checks. Describing white privilege makes one newly accountable.” - *Peggy McIntosh, White Privilege and Male Privilege, 1988*

I think it's best to think of it against the Wheel of Privilege shown opposite. You're not fully privileged or marginalised, you have privilege in some senses and not others. It's not a binary value, you're somewhere on the spectrum of privilege for all the slices of the wheel.

How do you know if you have the knapsack? Again Peggy McIntosh comes to the rescue, but I'm taking these selected examples from Layla F Saad's [Me and White Supremacy](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/46002342-me-and-white-supremacy) and adapting them slightly. These examples should give you an idea how the invisible knapsack manifests but there are many more:
- I did not have to educate my children to be aware of systemic racism for their daily physical protection
- I do not have to worry about the threat of violence directly caused by my identity
- I can be sure that if I need legal or medical help, my identity will not work against me
- There are no legal restrictions or cultural norms that restrict my romantic/sexual orientation or gender identity
- I can go into a book shop and count on finding the writing of my race (or general group) represented, into a supermarket and find the staple foods that fit with my cultural traditions, into a hairdresser's and find someone who can deal with my hair
- When visiting somewhere I can enter the building without requiring assistance

It's not your fault if these things apply to you, but you are privileged if they do. I highly recommend you try out [this tool developed by the BBC](https://www.bbc.co.uk/creativediversity/allyshipapp/#/) which gives a good indication where you are on the scale of privilege or how these factors affect your position on the hill.

## A guide to denial
You're being accused of being privileged. How do you react to that? One of the most common reactions is denial.

If you want an introduction to common types of privilege denial I highly recommend the Interlude in Akala's [Natives: Race and Class in the Ruins of Empire](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/36352480-natives). I'll list some versions of his common denials here, in the hopes that it helps you think of privilege in a different way. These are things you might think when Kate suggests you are privileged...

*If we just stop talking about (X)ism it will go away*

Imagine if a group of software engineers decided the best way to solve a problem is to ignore it completely and hope it works itself out rather than to discuss, confront and/or challenge the problem. For Kate, if you ignore the difference between you and her on the hill, what good does that do anyone? It might help you feel better about your situation but ignoring a problem doesn't make it go away.

*Why can't you just get over it? It's all in the past*

Which parts of the past are we allowed to remember? Should we get over Shakespeare, get over Churchill, or shall we just get over the parts that are uncomfortable or were less favourable to underprivileged groups? Who gets to decide what to remember and what isn't worth remembering? Who does that benefit? You and Kate both have a path to follow on this hill, why is it that you want to forget what she has gone through to get where she is?

*But what about `<insert any injustice here>`?*

Talking about one type of privilege does not take away from the existence of others. If we talk about Kate's situation on this hill it doesn't stop the fact that LGBT+ people are routinely discriminated against across the world. Or that racism is a real problem for a lot of people. What about X? is just a deflection tactic, it doesn't take the issue away.

*Stop making excuses*

If I were to ask why the North of England is generally poorer than the South would you give an answer that explains the socio-economic history of the South compared to the North, the politics of the North-South divide and how history shaped the division? Or would you say people from the North are just making excuses? People from the North are generally less intelligent, less able and put in less effort, you could say. Which one would it be? Why is it that you think Kate may be making excuses and you're not?

### Privilige as a Platform - using it for good
With great power comes great responsibility - and so it is that your invisible knapsack has the tools to help those without them.

Once you wake up to your privilege you can use it. Take the experiences of this person ([taken from here](https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/diversity-beyond-lip/9781523098712/xhtml/12_chapter03.html)) who came to realise how they had been in denial and how they could help. It's a longish read but worth it.

>At fifty years of age, finally, I woke up to my White Male Privilege. And I’m glad to tell you—especially if you’re white or male or both, like me—waking up has been a growth experience. (...) All my life I told myself I was one of “the good guys.” I was born and raised in Massachusetts in a working-class family. I’ve adhered all my life to progressive values. I came out as gay in my early twenties. I attended a top-notch university and traveled the world extensively working for economic and social development. I’ve volunteered for causes like cancer research, LGBT youth suicide prevention, and social services for newly arrived immigrants.
>
>The “bad people” propagate racism and male chauvinism. I’ve always distinguished myself from “those people” with “not me” rationalizations: not me, my family worked hard just to make a modest living. Not me, I’m a lifelong progressive. Not me, I’m gay—one of the oppressed. Not me, I’ve worked to help the less fortunate. Not me, I’m a deeply empathetic person—ask anyone! (...)
>
>My awakening began gradually until one day the shades were thrown open. I was conducting a leadership development workshop for a client. (...) After I presented the concept (Trustworthy Citizen), one member of the group spoke up, pointing out that the word “citizen” aligned with white supremacist rhetoric and that she and others in the room were uncomfortable with my language. I could feel the tips of my ears burning as I attempted a reassuring reply. (...)
>
>My assurances did not have the effect I hoped for, and for a few minutes I presided over what was for me an extremely awkward and uncomfortable conversation, ending with my retreating to this: “I feel uncomfortable facilitating this conversation, especially as a middle-aged white guy.”
>
>That was it. That was the moment I fully woke up, albeit in a daze. I was fully exposed to the light of my privileged effort to escape the moment because it was uncomfortable. (...)
>
>Waking up is only the first step. The ability to see my White Male Privilege is the beginning of a longer process. Since that moment of new clarity, I’ve taken responsibility for learning some new language. I’ve begun searching for resources to educate myself. (...) 
>
>White Male Privilege is not a mark of shame. White Male Privilege is a social reality that’s been passed down to us and that we perpetuate through complacent slumber. When we first wake up to it, we might feel defensive or guilty. We’re also human beings, and so those feelings are understandable, but defensiveness and guilt don’t serve us if we then wish to contribute to positive change.
>
>I am awake and I am seeking to learn what I can do with that clarity. I’m ready to find ways to use owning the fact of my White Male Privilege as a tool for doing even more good.
