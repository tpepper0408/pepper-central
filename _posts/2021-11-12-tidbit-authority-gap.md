TIDbits (Tech Inclusion & Diversity Bits) are short introductions to concepts related to Inclusion and Diversity the Tech Industry . 

This is a quick exploration of **The Authority Gap** which is an experience common to many underprivileged groups where their authority in a leadership position is questioned in explicit and implicit ways because of their identity.

<!-- more -->

As is the case with a lot of people, I didn't explicitly experience resistance to my role until I was in a position of authority at work as a technical lead. First of all, it presented itself in a way that made me think that I was at fault. It was easy to think that some people weren't taking my opinions seriously because I was unproven. Some assumed I was in a different role from what I was, in my mind it was because I wasn't holding myself in the way I should be. Secondly, I felt this was normal for somebody joining a new role. Even though I was the most qualified at that point to take on the role, my imposter syndrome kicked in which I confused for a long time with an invisible wall that I have come to understand as **The Authority Gap**. So what is it?

### Mary Ann Sieghart

In her book [The Authority Gap](https://www.penguin.co.uk/books/1120787/the-authority-gap/9780857527561.html), Sieghart explains how women, to this day, have to justify their authority in most situations where they are afforded it.

### How the gap presented itself
I am also vulnerable to the authority gap as an LGBTQ+ person in tech. Reading through Sieghart's work resonated with me in a way that made me realise it's something I have been experiencing as well. She mentions the authority chasms due to race, class, disability and sexuality as deserving their own books[^1] and I can feel these books needing to come soon. Personally, the gap came up most recently when my role involved *architecture*, which in the tech industry is possibly the last bastion of the straight white man. I'm no stranger to what an architect does and what kinds of concerns they have, but I'd never been included in that group before and I immediately felt a difference from some members compared to others. The people that had worked with me before immediately respected my opinions and treated me as an equal mind in the forum, those that hadn't treated my opinions as questionable even when I knew I was correct. I could have mounds of evidence to back my opinions up but I needed more to be taken seriously. I found myself excluded from some meetings with no explanation when I was technically responsible for the product. I felt it again when I raised something to the leadership team and was immediately sidelined so they could talk to more important people about it, even though I was the one that brought it up and knew most about the situation. In short, it's something to watch out for and something that will come up more and more as we elucidate the unconscious bias that exists for many marginalised groups in the industry and I'd encourage everyone to share their experiences in this space.
